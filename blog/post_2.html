Most Popular Spring Weekend Artists

Prakrit Baruah, November 2020

Every college has traditions that students boast about and alums reminisce over. For Brown, that tradition is Spring Weekend. Every year, thousands of students pour into Brown’s Main Green to listen to incredible musicians. Ray Charles, James Brown, Bob Dylan, Bruce Springsteen, U2, R.E.M, The Black Keys, Childish Gambino, Kendrick, Erykah Badu; it’s hard to believe that some of these people bothered coming to Providence, Rhode Island. Naturally, I’ve wondered whether I timed my further education well. How did my Spring Weekends compare to those of past classes? Which year had the best lineup? And how famous were these artists when they came to Brown?

To answer these questions, I created graphs that measure a proxy for popularity that was initially used in a FiveThirtyEight article ranking Superbowl half-time shows. Popularity is defined with respect to the number of songs an artist releases that reach the Billboard Top 100 charts. Popularity points scale so that a song ranked by Billboard as No. 1 is worth 100 points and one ranked No. 100 is worth 1 point. An artist’s accumulation of points illustrates their popularity over time.

The first graph shows the most popular Spring Weekend lineups to have ever graced the green. Each segment of each bar represents a different artist and the total points they racked up over their career. You can hover over the bar to read artist’s names (*these graphs won’t work well on mobile).

Unsurprisingly, most of these artists didn’t perform anywhere near the peak of their popularity. Many artists came before they had ever entered into the Billboard Top 100. Below, I show the most interesting Spring Weekend lineups. The greyed out area indicates the time before their appearance at Brown.

It’s worth noting that this method is an imperfect measure of an artist’s fame or talent. Firstly, this method biases against more recent artists. Older performers have more time to amass top hits. Secondly, artists can be enormously popular without ever reaching the Billboard top 100. Ella Fitzgerald is a household name but barely figures in terms of popularity points. Thirdly, the music industry and people’s music-listening habits have changed drastically in the last 50 years. This affects who we listen to and how we listen to them. Quantitative comparisons between The Yardbirds and Young Thug should therefore be taken with a grain of salt. However, it’s safe to say that the class of ‘68 had it pretty good.
